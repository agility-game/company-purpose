## Types of Company Purpose

### Big-P Purpose vs. Medium-P Purpose
Company purposes can be categorized into two primary types: **Big-P purpose** and **Medium-P purpose**. Big-P purpose refers to a company's overarching role in the world, encapsulating its long-term vision and mission. For instance, AT&T’s purpose statement, “We create connection,” reflects a broad aspiration that is both relatable and consistent with the company's products and services, providing clarity on its fundamental reason for existence [16](https://www.bain.com/insights/how-to-measure-the-metrics-that-matter/) [17](tion/news/how-purpose-drives-performance-insights-professor-rodolphe-durand). Conversely, Medium-P purpose is more focused on the specific role a company plays in the lives of its customers. This type of purpose is often tailored to individual brands within a corporate portfolio. For example, Kimberly-Clark’s Huggies brand articulates its Medium-P purpose as “Helping to navigate the unknowns of babyhood,” which addresses the unique needs of first-time parents, creating a connection beyond just product offerings [18](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/purpose-shifting-from-why-to-how).

### Purpose-Driven Business
A **purpose-driven business** embodies a commitment that transcends mere profit generation. These companies aim to tackle societal issues and contribute positively to environmental sustainability while still being financially viable. Their core mission is integrated into all facets of the organization, influencing decision-making and operations. This genuine purpose is critical in distinguishing between authentic corporate social responsibility and practices such as “purpose-washing” or “greenwashing,” which merely project an image of responsibility without substantial commitment [3](https://pressbooks.hccfl.edu/businessethics/chapter/business-ethics-over-time/).

### Dimensions of Corporate Purpose
Research indicates that corporate purpose can be analyzed through various dimensions, including regulatory-oriented approaches, business-oriented approaches, and organizational culture-oriented approaches. These frameworks help businesses align their corporate purpose with social and environmental objectives, ensuring that their operations do not cause socio-environmental harm while also maximizing profits. A successful corporate purpose should be meaningful, authentic, and accountable, contributing to both financial success and societal benefits [2](https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/jmh.2006.15812baa.001/full/html) [7](https://www.yourthoughtpartner.com/blog/purpose-statement).

## Challenges and Criticisms
The pursuit of a clearly defined company purpose is fraught with challenges and criticisms, primarily revolving around authenticity, alignment, and the practical implications of implementation.

### Authenticity and Credibility
One significant challenge is the struggle for authenticity in expressing corporate purpose. Many organizations articulate their purpose through mission statements, but often, these ideals fail to resonate within the organizational culture. This disconnect frequently stems from a lack of genuine commitment among leaders or skepticism about the practicality of these values [8](https://www2.deloitte.com/us/en/pages/operations/articles/value-of-corporate-purpose.html). When daily operations contradict professed missions—such as when shortcuts are taken—trust and cohesion within the team can erode, leading to what is commonly referred to as "purpose-washing," where organizations are accused of superficial engagement with their stated values [9](https://hbr.org/2021/05/whats-your-customers-purpose).

### Alignment with Strategy
Furthermore, aligning corporate purpose with overall business strategy presents another layer of difficulty. Companies may find that their social and environmental impacts, strategic objectives, and core purposes are misaligned, necessitating a hard look at the choices made regarding market competition [23](https://www.entrepreneur.com/growing-a-business/prioritizing-purpose-over-profit-is-key-to-consistent/472953). Addressing this misalignment can be uncomfortable but is essential for developing an effective strategy that truly reflects the company's mission. Questions surrounding the congruence of strategic pillars with corporate purpose can stimulate valuable introspection, but they also highlight the friction between short-term gains and long-term commitment to purpose [23](https://www.entrepreneur.com/growing-a-business/prioritizing-purpose-over-profit-is-key-to-consistent/472953).

### Stakeholder Theory and Balancing Interests
The criticisms of stakeholder theory also complicate the discourse surrounding corporate purpose. While the theory advocates for a balanced approach to managing the interests of various stakeholders—ranging from shareholders to the community—critics argue that the definitions of who constitutes a stakeholder and how to prioritize their interests remain inadequately articulated [10](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/competitive-advantage-purpose-how-businesses-can-win-beyond-g-kjnif). This lack of clarity can lead to confusion and inconsistency in decision-making, with organizations struggling to find a clear rationale for balancing stakeholder interests effectively.

### Operational Challenges
Additionally, operationalizing purpose can lead to inefficiencies and unintended consequences. A superficial or "patch-job" approach to integrating purpose into business operations often yields poor outcomes, such as alienating customers and demotivating employees [9](https://hbr.org/2021/05/whats-your-customers-purpose). Companies must take a strategic approach to ensure that their business portfolios align with their stated purposes and that they refrain from simply accepting existing conditions as unchangeable [9](https://hbr.org/2021/05/whats-your-customers-purpose).

### Holistic Performance Measurement
Finally, the shift towards more holistic performance management metrics, which assess not only results but also adherence to company culture and values, presents its own set of challenges. While this approach seeks to create a more comprehensive evaluation of employee contributions, it can lead to confusion if not implemented with clear and easily understood structures [11](https://www.businessethicsnetwork.org/the-role-of-business-and-ethics-in-modern-business/). Employees may find themselves demotivated by systems that lack transparency, further complicating the integration of corporate purpose into daily operations.
